The Heston Model

Introduction

The Heston model is a popular framework in quantitative finance for modeling the stochastic volatility of an asset. It assumes that the variance of the asset price follows a mean-reverting stochastic process, leading to a more realistic representation of market behaviors such as volatility clustering.

Deriving the Asset Pricing Equation

The asset price \( S_t \) evolves as:

\[\partial S_{t} = r\cdot S_{t}\cdot \partial t + \sqrt{v_{t}}\cdot S_t\cdot \partial W_{t}^S\]

The variance \( v_t \) follows the dynamics:

\[\partial v_t = \kappa \cdot (\theta - v_{t})\cdot \partial t + \sigma_{v} \cdot \sqrt{v_{t}} \cdot \partial W_{t}^v\]

where \( \rho = \text{corr}(W_t^S, W_t^v) \). Solving these equations under the risk-neutral measure yields the pricing formula for derivatives.
